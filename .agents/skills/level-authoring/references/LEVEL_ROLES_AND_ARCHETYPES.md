# Level Roles and Archetypes

Use one primary role per level. A secondary role is fine, but it should support the primary role instead of competing with it.

## Roles

### Teach

Introduce one rule or interaction with a readable first move and a forgiving consequence. Keep the board dense enough to feel like real play.

### Practice

Repeat a known skill in a slightly different geometry. Practice levels should create confidence without becoming filler.

### Release

Lower tension after pressure or exam levels while still preserving the rescue identity.

### Pressure

Make one known skill urgent through water, vine, dock, or target placement.

### Choice

Offer multiple plausible paths or target orders where the player must compare tradeoffs.

### Exam

Combine learned skills without adding a new mechanic. The question should be "can you use what you know?" rather than "can you parse new noise?"

### Spectacle

Create a memorable, readable rescue beat that shows the game's identity quickly.

### Recovery

Let the player regain confidence after a heavy or punishing level.

## Pacing guidance

Avoid long runs of pressure and exam levels. A healthy sequence alternates learning, pressure, mastery, and relief.

Good campaign rhythm usually looks like:

- teach,
- practice,
- pressure,
- release or choice,
- exam,
- recovery.

Do not make every level the cleverest possible version of its idea. Some levels exist to make the next hard level fair.

## Archetype checks

For any authored level, write the role in `meta.notes` when it is not obvious from `intent`.

Before finalizing, ask:

- What is the one primary role?
- What player behavior does this role reward?
- What fair mistake does it punish?
- Does any secondary mechanic distract from the role?
- Does this level's role make sense after the previous level and before the next one?
