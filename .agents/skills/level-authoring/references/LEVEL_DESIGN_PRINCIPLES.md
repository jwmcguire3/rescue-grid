# Level Design Principles

## Core identity

Rescue Grid levels must prove that the game is a rescue-order puzzle under calm urgency.

Keep these principles visible during authoring:

1. Acting advances danger; thinking is free.
2. Dock tension feels self-authored.
3. Rescue order is the central puzzle.
4. Rescue extraction feels different from generic completion.

Desired player language:

- "I picked the wrong rescue first."
- "I overfilled the dock."
- "I had time to think."
- "I saved the puppy because I planned the route."

Avoid levels that make players say:

- "The timer got me."
- "I got unlucky."
- "It is just tray sort with puppies."
- "I opened the puppy and the game still said no."

## Rescue-first construction

Place targets and rescue routes before filling the board. A level should be readable as a rescue problem before it reads as a debris-clearing problem.

Good levels usually start from:

- the target location,
- the route or routes to expose the target,
- the water or vine pressure that makes order matter,
- the dock consequences of taking those routes,
- the wrong-but-tempting move that teaches attribution.

Then add surrounding debris, blockers, and negative space.

## Action pressure

Water and vine pressure must be action-based and forecasted. The player should feel pressure while choosing actions, not while thinking.

Use pressure to clarify priority:

- lower or endangered targets should force rescue-order decisions,
- vine should add warned future route cost,
- water should create triage, not surprise failure.

Do not use hidden timers, sudden unwarned growth, or water budgets that leave no fair margin for correct play.

## Dock tension

Dock tension should feel self-authored. The player should understand how their chosen group sizes and types created pressure.

Use dock pressure to shape route decisions:

- 2-size groups can be useful but costly,
- 4- and 5-size groups leave remainders,
- complete triples in the removed group are free from dock insertion,
- tempting clears can create rack problems while better rescue clears stay disciplined.

Avoid making dock failure come from sheer clutter. If the board fails because every move is messy, the level is muddy, not clever.

## Fair failure

The expected fail mode should be a teachable mistake, not a gotcha.

Fair fail modes include:

- saving the safe target before the urgent target,
- chasing dock value before opening a rescue route,
- ignoring a warned vine growth,
- spending actions on a visually tempting but low-value lane,
- overfilling the dock through avoidable sequencing.

Unfair fail modes include:

- needing hidden spawn luck,
- water reaching a target with no readable warning,
- extraction appearing complete but not counting,
- blockers or empty cells hiding the intended route,
- one correct path with no readable first move.

## Common design mistakes

- Making the target decorative while the real puzzle is generic clearing.
- Letting the dock overshadow rescue after the opening dock lessons.
- Teaching ice on a route where ice can be ignored.
- Placing vine growth where the player never sees or cares about it.
- Creating three-target boards where one order is obviously dominant and no triage remains.
- Lowering density so much that the board becomes a diagram.
- Depending on assisted spawn to make the intended route work.
- Raising difficulty by adding clutter instead of sharpening the decision.
